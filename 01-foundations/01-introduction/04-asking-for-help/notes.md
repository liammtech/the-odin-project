# 04. ASKING FOR HELP

## Introduction

- Along your journey you will inevitably need to ask questions
- Seems simple, but being able to ask a well formed question is imperative
- In essence, "help others help you!"

## Lesson overview

- Explain techniques for asking programming questions
- Explain and avoid qualities of "bad" questions
- Ask effective and well-formed questions

## Tips for getting the best help possible

### Always provide youe code and the surrounding contextr
- When asking a question, you must provide:
    - Your code
    - Error message
    - Terminal command
    - Server output
    - Any other relevant details
- You should provide as much context as possible
- Zero in on the specific problem e.g. a specific function or line in your code
- This makes it easier for others to help you
- A lack of detail can make it difficult (and become frustrating) for those trying to help

### Ask about the problem at hand, not the solution itself
A lot of learners ask exactly how to approach a given task or assignment in this
curriculum, for example:

- "How do I complete Step 5 of the Rock Paper Scissors assignment"?

Keep in mind that you're supposed to figure out how to solve the assignments yourself, and
figuring out an approach is essential to your learning journey. A much better question
might look like this:

- "Hey, I'm trying to return a string that displays the winner in Rock Paper Scissors, but
I'm getting a syntax error on line 12. How can I fix this? Here's my code."

In sharing your attempt, people know what you've tried and won't suggest things that might
not have worked out for you. It also allows them to debug your current iteration of code,
rather than sending you down a path that has you start all over again when you might be
very close to a solution.

- If you're completely stuck, it's fine to let people know
- Asking where you can start empowers you to be able to resolve issues on your own
- May empower others to do the same
- If you have pseudo code, share that too

### Don't take asking for more context to heart

- People who volunteer in coding communities are here to help
- A question you think is obvious might not be, if you're being asked to elaborate
- Something that may seem "obvious" to a beginner may not be to an expert
- Experts know about many more situations that could cause an issue, and don't want to
send you down the wrong path
- People take time out of their day to help; make it as easy as possible for them
- There's probably a good reason they need more info, so trust their judgment
- Many are unpaid volunteers, not obligated to help

## KNOWLEDGE CHECK

- What is the "XY Problem"?

When you ask about your attempted solution rather than your actual problem, and send those
trying to help on a wild goose chase. In other words, providing too little or poor 
context.

- What 5 things should your question include?

1. Provide a link to the lesson/project in the curriculum
2. Provide code, pseudo code, or other relevant information
3. Explain the problem
4. Describe what you are expecting
5. Summarise what you have tried
